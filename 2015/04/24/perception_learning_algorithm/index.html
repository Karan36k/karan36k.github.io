Perception Learning Algorithm

Posted on 2015-04-24 Edited on 2020-04-09 Views:
Symbols count in article: 6.3k Reading time ≈ 6 mins.

PLA (Perception Learning Algorithm) is suitable for two-dimensional and high-dimensional linear divideable problems. The answer to the question is only to agree or disagree.

Examples

Banks can determine whether to issue credit cards to customers based on their personal information. ** Abstract customers as a vector $ X $, including name, age, annual income, number of liabilities, etc. At the same time, the weight vector occupied by each attribute is set to $ W $. A relatively high weight is set for positively related attributes, such as annual income, and a lower weight is set for negatively related attributes, such as the number of liabilities. $ y $ indicates whether you want the user to issue a credit card. ** By taking the inner product of $ X $ and $ W $ minus a threshold, if it is positive, agree to issue a credit card, otherwise no credit card will be issued. We assume that there is a mapping $ f $ from $ X $ to $ Y $. The PLA algorithm is used to simulate this mapping, so that the function found is as similar as possible to $ f $, at least in the known data set ( That is, the sample is consistent.

! [img] (http://q6gm8fomw.bkt.clouddn.com/gitpage/Hsuan-Tien_Lin/perception-learning-algorithm/1.png)

** PLA algorithm is used to find the vector $ W $ **, so that the judgment made by the machine in the known data is the same as the real data. When $ X $ is a two-dimensional vector, it is equivalent to drawing a straight line on a plane to divide all points into two parts, one part agrees to send, and the other part disagrees. The inner product can be expressed as:
$$
\ begin {eqnarray}
h (x) & = & sign ((\ sum \ limits_ {i = 1} ^ {d} W_i X_i) -threshold) \
& = & sign ((\ sum \ limits_ {i = 1} ^ {d} W_i X_i) + \ underbrace {(-threshold)} _ {W_0} \ times \ underbrace {(+ 1)} _ {X_0}) \
& = & sign (\ sum \ limits_ {i = 0} ^ {d} W_i X_i) \
& = & sign (W ^ TX)
\ end {eqnarray}
$$

Where $ X_0 = 1, W_0 = -threshold $

Value range of $ y_s $: $ \ {+ 1, -1 } $, ($ y_s $ represents the value of $ y $ in the sample, which is used to input to the algorithm for adjustment)

Combined with the example in the text: $ y_s = 1 $ means that in the given sample data, a credit card is issued to the user, and $ y_s = -1 $ means that it is not issued.

PLA first assumes that $ W_0 $ is a vector $ \ vec {0} $, then finds a point that does not satisfy the condition, adjusts the value of $ W $, and sequentially ** iterates all sample data ** so that the two parts can be completely separated in the end .

At this time, the adjustment coefficient is $ y_s (i) $ of the sample, and the adjusted $ W_ {t + 1} = W_t + y_s (i) X_i $, the subscript $ t of $ W $, t + 1 $ indicates the number of adjustments, schematic diagram:

For linearly separable data sets, the PLA algorithm is convergent

! [img] (http://q6gm8fomw.bkt.clouddn.com/gitpage/Hsuan-Tien_Lin/perception-learning-algorithm/5.png)

** Increase the inner product of two vectors: **

  1. ** The smaller the angle between the two vectors **
  2. ** Or the length of the vector increases **

! [img] (http://q6gm8fomw.bkt.clouddn.com/gitpage/Hsuan-Tien_Lin/perception-learning-algorithm/6.png)

Teacher’s ppt on $ || W_ {t + 1} || ^ 2 \ le || W_t || ^ 2 + max \ {1 \ le i \ le n \ \ | \ \ || y_i X_i || ^ 2 } $ Among them, the range of $ y_i $ is $ \ {+ 1, -1 } $

So $ || W_ {t + 1} || ^ 2 \ le || W_t || ^ 2 + max \ {1 \ le i \ le n \ \ | \ \ || X_i || ^ 2 } $

This shows that after each adjustment, the length of the vector is limited to increase. May wish

Bring in the previous formula to get:

$$
\ begin {eqnarray}
\ frac {|| W_ {t + 1} || ^ 2} {|| W_ {t} || ^ 2} & \ le & \ frac {|| W_ {t} || ^ 2 + max || y_n x_n || ^ 2} {|| W_ {t} || ^ 2} \
& = & \ frac {|| W_ {t} || ^ 2 + max || x_n || ^ 2} {|| W_ {t} || ^ 2} \
& = & 1+ \ frac {R ^ 2} {|| W (t) || ^ 2}
\ end {eqnarray}
$$

So $ W_t $ eventually converged, and so far it has been proved that the PLA algorithm can finally stop.

The number of times the algorithm needs to be adjusted

The following two inequalities can be obtained from the above process:

$$
\ begin {eqnarray}
W_f ^ t W_t & = & W_f ^ t (W_ {t-1} + y_s (t-1) X_ {t-1}) \
& = & W_f ^ tW_ {t-1} + y_s (t-1) W_f ^ tX (t-1) \
& \ ge & W_f ^ tW_ {t-1} + min (yW_f ^ tX) \
& \ ge & W_f ^ tW_ {t-2} +2 , min (yW_f ^ tX) \
& \ cdots & \
& \ ge & W_f ^ tW_0 + t , min (yW_f ^ tX) \
& = & t , min (yW_f ^ tX)
\ end {eqnarray}
$$

$$
\ begin {eqnarray}
|| W_t || ^ 2 & \ le & || W_ {t-1} || ^ 2 + max (|| X || ^ 2) \
& \ le & || W_ {t-2} || ^ 2 + 2 , max (|| X || ^ 2) \
& \ cdots & \
& \ le & || W_0 || ^ 2 + t , max (|| X || ^ 2) \
& = & t , max (|| X || ^ 2)
\ end {eqnarray}
$$

Then look at this formula: $ \ frac {W_f ^ t W_t} {|| W_f ^ t || \ || W_t ||} \ ge \ frac {t \ min (yW_f ^ tX)} {|| W_f ^ t || \ sqrt {t , (max (|| X ||)) ^ 2}} = \ sqrt {t} , \ frac {min (yW_f ^ tX)} {|| W_f ^ t || max (|| X ||)} $

According to the maximum cosine value of 1, you can get $ \ frac {W_f ^ tW_t} {|| W_f ^ t || \ || W_t ||} \ le 1 $, so we get the number of adjustments: $ t \ le \ frac {|| W_f ^ t || (max (|| X ||)) ^ 2} {(min (yW_f ^ tX)) ^ 2} = {R ^ 2 \ over \ rho ^ 2} $.
